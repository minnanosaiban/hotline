On the Litigation over ENEOS's Internal Whistleblower System

I am an employee who used the company's internal whistleblower hotline.

What this website calls into question is the company's conduct after the report was filed. The company excluded the whistleblower from information, and disclosed nothing but information that could not be substantiated. In the text below, I also refer to myself — the person who filed the report — as "the whistleblower."

1. Leaving the subject of the investigation vague

What I reported was the problem that "we are paying an overseas consumption tax (GST) that we have no obligation to pay." Yet the subject of the investigation was vague, and even what the finding applied to was unclear. The crucial question — whether the overseas consumption tax actually had to be paid — was never presented as a finding of the investigation.

2. Information that cannot be substantiated

On receiving the report, the company told the whistleblower that "there had been an amendment to the law," and an overseas subsidiary remitted funds to the head office, described as "having received a refund on behalf of the head office." Yet neither that "amendment to the law" nor the "refund" can be confirmed as fact. All that the company presented to the whistleblower was information that cannot be substantiated.

3. Turning the GST payment into a contractual term

After receiving the report that overseas consumption tax was being paid, and even though a Japanese company has no obligation to pay overseas consumption tax, the company amended the contract into a form under which "the overseas company may charge GST to ENEOS." This measure was never disclosed to the whistleblower.

The court's decision 　The point at issue is that, regarding the contract amendment made after the report, ENEOS "did not notify the whistleblower of the corrective measure it had taken in response to the report." The court reasoned that, although the contract contained no provision on overseas consumption tax, ENEOS is presumed to have reached an "agreement to pay overseas consumption tax" with its business partner, and that, because such an agreement exists, paying the tax is not a breach of contract. If there is no breach in the first place, then there is also no "corrective measure for a breach" that needed to be conveyed to the whistleblower — on that basis, the court rejected the argument challenging the failure to notify.

Three explanations that
justify the payment

I lost the lawsuit. Yet through the litigation, one fact became clear: regarding the payment of an overseas consumption tax that there was no obligation to pay, ENEOS had told the whistleblower that "there had been an amendment to the law" and that "the overseas subsidiary received a refund on its behalf." Including these, the explanations offered to justify the payment are the following three.

1. "Because there was an amendment to the law"
— but that amendment has still not been identified

The company told the whistleblower, "because there was an amendment to the law."
— Yet which law, and as of when, that "amendment" refers to has still not been identified. (Even when the whistleblower searched, it could not be found.)

2. "We received a refund of the overseas consumption tax"
— but the only record is a remittance "subsidiary head office"

The company presented to the whistleblower, as the solution, that "the overseas subsidiary received a refund of the overseas consumption tax on behalf of the head office."
— Yet the only thing that can be confirmed in the records is a "remittance from the overseas subsidiary to the head office"; no record showing that "the overseas subsidiary actually collected the money from an outside party" can be found.

3. "Payment based on an agreement"
— but no record of any agreement is shown at all

Regarding the payment of an overseas consumption tax that need not be paid, the court presumed it to be a "payment based on an agreement."
— Yet the contract contains no provision to pay overseas consumption tax, and no record showing an agreement is presented at all. Even if an agreement had truly existed, paying an unnecessary overseas consumption tax on the basis of an unrecorded agreement is itself a problem of internal control.

A second report,
and a new problem

Regarding the remittance from the overseas subsidiary to the head office that was presented as the solution, I filed a fresh report, because the remittance is suspected of being improper. The company, however, notified me only that the matter was "already resolved," without presenting any investigation findings. Just as with my first report, this second report too was left with no clear answer as to whether any wrongdoing had occurred. Such a response plainly contravenes the Guidelines under the Whistleblower Protection Act.

Commentary on the Guidelines under the Whistleblower Protection Act — English translation of the Japanese original

As examples of cases where there is a "legitimate reason" not to conduct an investigation, one may consider, for instance, cases where information is provided about a matter that has already been resolved, or cases where it is difficult to confirm the facts because the whistleblower cannot be contacted. As to whether a matter is "already resolved," the whistleblower's understanding of the resolution and the business operator's understanding may not coincide; the determination of whether or not it is resolved is required to be made as objectively as possible.

Moreover, President Miyata, invoking the former president's sexual-harassment scandal, had been urging employees to use the internal whistleblower system under the banner of "re-instilling compliance." My second report was a response to that very call. To urge use of the system, yet to respond to those who use it with nothing more than "already resolved" — this is conduct that contravenes the Guidelines under the Whistleblower Protection Act.

And if such a response is to be treated as raising no problem, then it means that no matter who files a report, it can be disposed of with the single phrase "already resolved." That, one may say, is a flaw in the Whistleblower Protection Act itself.
